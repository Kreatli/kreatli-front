# CPM Explained (Cost Per Mille / Per 1,000 Views)

**CPM** stands for *cost per mille* — Latin for "per thousand." In clipping and content rewards, CPM means **payment tied to every 1,000 views** a clip earns, rather than a fixed fee for the editing work itself.

Creators funding CPM campaigns and clippers earning from them operate on performance economics. That is fundamentally different from hiring a freelance clip editor on **Kreatli Marketplace**, where pay is agreed upfront per project regardless of how the algorithm performs.

## Why it matters for creators and clippers

CPM sounds simple — "$5 CPM" implies $5 per thousand views — but clipping campaigns add layers:

- **View verification** — platforms may count only "qualified" views
- **Caps and floors** — earnings may stop after a budget is exhausted
- **Platform cuts** — the clipper's take is rarely the headline CPM rate
- **Volatility** — a 60-second edit that took two hours can earn $0.50 or $500

Creators using CPM models are buying **distribution at scale**. Clippers accepting CPM are taking **algorithm risk**. Neither is wrong — but neither is the same as fixed-price freelance editing.

## How CPM works in clipping campaigns

A typical content-rewards flow:

1. A creator or brand sets a **reward pool** (e.g. $1,000)
2. Clippers edit campaign footage and **post on their accounts**
3. The platform tracks views per clip
4. Earnings calculate as: `(views ÷ 1,000) × CPM rate`, until the pool runs out

Example: A clip earns 50,000 views at $4 CPM → gross $200 — but only if the campaign budget, eligibility rules, and platform fee structure allow the full payout.

## CPM vs. fixed project pay

| Factor | CPM / content rewards | Fixed project pay (Kreatli Marketplace) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Pay basis | Views and campaign rules | Agreed deliverables |
| Income predictability | Low | High for completed work |
| Who posts | Usually the clipper | Creator on owned channels |
| Best for | Viral distribution plays | Consistent clip pipelines |
| Editor risk | Algorithm-dependent | Scope-defined upfront |

A freelance clipper charging **$30 per podcast clip** knows the job pays $30 when delivered. A CPM clipper has no such guarantee.

## Examples

**High CPM, low total:** A clip hits $8 CPM but only reaches 800 views → $6.40 earned for an hour of editing.

**Low CPM, high volume:** A clip earns $2 CPM across 2 million views → $4,000 — rare, but what CPM optimists chase.

**Fixed project pay:** A creator hires an editor for 15 clips at $25 each → $375 total, paid on delivery regardless of TikTok performance.

## FAQ

### What is a good CPM rate for clipping?

Rates vary by platform, niche, and campaign. Consumer-facing clipping programs often advertise **$1–$10+ CPM**, but effective take-home depends on fees, view qualification, and competition. Treat advertised CPM as a ceiling under ideal conditions, not a wage.

### Do creators pay CPM when hiring an editor?

Not on editor marketplaces. When you **hire** a clipper on Kreatli Marketplace, you pay a **fixed project rate** — the editor does not earn per view on your channel. CPM is a content-rewards concept, not a freelance hiring model.

### Can clippers do both CPM campaigns and freelance work?

Yes. Some editors run content-rewards campaigns for upside while taking Kreatli Marketplace jobs for stable income. The models are complementary when you treat CPM as speculative and project pay as your baseline.
