# What Is B-Roll?

**B-roll** is supplementary footage cut over or between primary footage (called **A-roll**) to add visual interest, context, or pacing. In short-form clips, B-roll covers talking-head moments, illustrates a point, or hides jump cuts — often in the first three seconds to stop the scroll.

Clip editors on **Kreatli Marketplace** use B-roll daily when turning podcasts, tutorials, and streams into vertical shorts that hold attention without a static frame.

## Why it matters for creators and clippers

Talking-head clips — a podcaster speaking directly to camera — lose viewers fast without visual variety. B-roll solves that:

- **Retention** — movement on screen keeps eyes engaged past the hook
- **Clarity** — show the product, stat, or location being discussed
- **Pacing** — cover edits where you removed pauses or mistakes (jump cuts)
- **Brand feel** — stock, custom, or screen recordings set tone

Creators who brief editors with B-roll direction ("use screen recordings when I mention the dashboard") get clips back that need fewer revision rounds.

## A-roll vs. B-roll

| Term | What it is | Example in a finance clip |
| --- | --- | --- |
| A-roll | Main footage — usually the speaker | Creator explaining compound interest |
| B-roll | Supporting footage layered on top | Stock chart animation, calculator app, dollar bills |

In a 45-second TikTok, A-roll might be 70% of runtime with B-roll filling the gaps — or B-roll might lead the hook before the speaker appears.

## Common B-roll sources for clips

1. **Stock libraries** — Artgrid, Storyblocks, Pexels (check licensing)
2. **Screen recordings** — app demos, dashboards, gameplay
3. **Creator-provided assets** — product shots, event footage, brand b-roll packs
4. **Generated visuals** — motion graphics, text overlays, chart animations
5. **Pull from the same session** — wide shots, reactions, or secondary angles from the original long-form recording

Clip editors often build a small B-roll library per client so week-two clips match week-one style.

## Examples

**Podcast clip:** The host tells a story about a restaurant failure. The editor layers B-roll of empty tables, a "CLOSED" sign, and a receipt close-up while the audio continues — the viewer stays engaged without staring at a waveform.

**Tutorial clip:** A SaaS founder explains a feature. B-roll shows the actual product UI with cursor movements synced to their narration.

**Gaming clip:** A streamer's rant plays over gameplay highlights from the same session — B-roll from the VOD, not unrelated stock.

## FAQ

### Do I need B-roll in every short-form clip?

No. Some clips work as pure talking-head with dynamic captions and zoom cuts — especially hot takes under 30 seconds. But clips over 45 seconds with a single static frame usually benefit from at least **2–3 B-roll inserts** in the first 15 seconds.

### Who sources B-roll — creator or editor?

Clarify in your job brief. Many creators provide a folder of brand assets; editors source licensed stock for the rest. On Kreatli Marketplace, specify: "editor sources stock" vs. "use only assets I provide" to avoid rights issues.

### Can B-roll cause copyright problems?

Yes. Random YouTube rips or unlicensed music behind B-roll get clips muted or taken down. Use royalty-free stock, creator-owned footage, or properly licensed assets. When in doubt, stick to screen recordings and original session footage.
