# Kreatli Marketplace vs. Hiring a Full-Time Editor

A **full-time in-house editor** is a salaried team member who works exclusively on your content. **Kreatli Marketplace** lets you hire freelance clip editors per project or ongoing pipeline — without payroll, benefits, or long-term headcount.

The right choice depends on your volume, budget, and how predictable your editing needs are month to month.

## Quick comparison

| Factor | Kreatli Marketplace | Full-time in-house editor |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Cost structure | Pay per job or ongoing freelance agreement | Salary + benefits + equipment (~$40k–$80k+/yr US) |
| Payment model | Project-based / milestone freelance pay | Fixed salary regardless of output volume |
| Vetting | Review clipper profiles before each hire | One hire decision — high stakes |
| Flexibility | Scale up or down weekly | Fixed capacity — harder to shrink |
| Onboarding speed | Days — post a job, hire this week | Weeks to months — recruiting + training |
| Best for | Growing channels with variable volume | High, stable daily editing workload |

## What is hiring in-house?

An in-house editor joins your team as an employee (or long-term contractor). They learn your brand deeply, attend meetings, use your tools, and handle editing as a core function — often across long-form, Shorts, thumbnails, and live turnaround.

The model works when editing is **always-on**: daily uploads, same-day stream highlights, or a media company with continuous production. You trade cash flexibility for dedicated capacity and institutional knowledge.

## Where in-house hiring works well

Full-time editors make sense when:

- You publish **daily or near-daily** and need someone in Slack at 9 a.m.
- Editing volume is **stable year-round** — not seasonal spikes
- You want one person embedded in **strategy, not just exports**
- Budget supports **$3,500–$7,000+/month** fully loaded for a junior-to-mid editor

Established podcast networks, daily news channels, and large creator teams often cross this threshold.

## Where in-house hiring falls short

For many growing creators, full-time is premature:

- **Fixed cost during slow months** — you pay salary even when you only need 8 clips, not 40
- **Recruiting risk** — a bad hire costs months, not one failed Fiverr gig
- **Niche mismatch** — your in-house generalist may not excel at Shorts hooks
- **Scaling friction** — doubling output may mean a second hire, not flexing freelancers

Channels in the "10–30 Shorts per week" range often sit in a gap: too much for DIY, not enough to justify a full salary plus benefits.

## How Kreatli Marketplace is different

Kreatli Marketplace gives creators **agency-style output without agency overhead**:

1. **Post clipping jobs** when volume spikes — pause when it does not.
2. **Hire specialists** — a podcast clipper this quarter, a gaming highlight editor next.
3. **Test before committing** — work with an editor for two weeks before any long-term arrangement.
4. **Pay for deliverables** — 20 clips at $X, not a fixed payroll line regardless of output.

Many creators treat their Kreatli Marketplace editor as a **fractional in-house clipper**: same person weekly, freelance economics.

## Who should use which?

**Choose a full-time in-house editor** if you have stable, high daily volume, budget for salary and benefits, and need someone embedded in your team full time.

**Choose Kreatli Marketplace** if you are scaling Shorts from podcasts or streams, volume fluctuates, or you want to hire clipping specialists without a 12-month payroll commitment.

**Hybrid path:** start on Kreatli Marketplace, then convert your best editor to full-time once volume justifies it — you will already know their work.

## Verdict

In-house editors are for **always-on production teams**. Kreatli Marketplace is for **flexible clip pipelines**. Most creators between $0 and $500k/year in revenue fit the freelance model better until editing becomes a full-time job by itself. When you are ready to test that path, [post a clipping job](/jobs/create) and hire without a recruiting cycle.
