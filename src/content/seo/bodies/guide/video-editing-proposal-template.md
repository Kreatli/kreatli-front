# How to Write a Winning Proposal for Video Editing Gigs

A good video editing proposal does not beg for work. It shows the creator that you understand the content, the deliverables, and the result they want. On **Kreatli Marketplace**, clip editors can stand out by writing specific proposals instead of sending the same generic "I can edit your videos" message to every job.

Use these templates for clipping jobs, TikTok/Reels edits, YouTube Shorts, podcast clips, gaming highlights, and recurring retainers.

## What a strong proposal includes

Your proposal should answer five questions quickly:

1. Did you read the job?
2. Have you edited similar content?
3. What would you do first?
4. What is included in your price?
5. What do you need from the creator?

Creators are looking for trust. Specificity creates trust.

## Read the job before you write

Before applying, underline the details that matter:

- Content type
- Source footage
- Deliverables
- Platform
- Timeline
- Budget
- Style references
- Required software or workflow
- Whether timestamps are provided

Then mirror two or three of those details in your proposal. This is not flattery; it proves you are responding to their job, not blasting the same message everywhere.

## Basic proposal structure

Use this format:

> Hi [name], I can help with [specific deliverable]. I have experience editing [similar content or sample type]. For your project, I would start by [specific approach]. My quote includes [deliverables, revisions, timeline]. To begin, I would need [files, examples, access, timestamps]. Here are relevant samples: [links].

That is enough for most applications. Keep it short, but make it real.

## Template for podcast clips

> Hi, I can help turn your podcast episodes into short vertical clips. For a first batch, I would pull the strongest standalone moments, tighten pauses, add readable burned-in captions, and export 9:16 files for Shorts, TikTok, and Reels. My quote includes [number] clips, [revision rounds], and delivery by [date]. If you have timestamps, I can move faster; if not, I can include source review. Relevant samples: [links].

Why it works: it mentions the actual workflow and gives the creator a clear next step.

## Template for TikTok or Reels editing

> Hi [name], I edit short-form clips for creators who need stronger hooks and cleaner pacing. For your TikTok/Reels batch, I would open each clip on the strongest line, remove dead air, add captions that stay inside safe zones, and deliver platform-ready MP4 files. I can start with a paid trial of [number] clips so we can lock the style before ongoing work. Samples: [links].

This works well when the job post mentions weekly content or brand consistency.

## Template for gaming highlights

> Hey, I can edit gaming highlights from your [game/platform] footage. I will keep the setup short, preserve the payoff, balance game and voice audio, and use captions/effects without covering important UI. For the trial batch, I can edit [number] clips from [timestamps/full VOD] and deliver versions for [platforms]. Gaming samples: [links].

For gaming jobs, mention the game or genre if you understand it. If you do not, be honest and focus on editing judgement.

## Template for faceless channel edits

> Hi, I can help edit faceless videos from your script and voiceover. I would sync visuals to the narration, use relevant B-roll or approved stock, add clean text callouts, and keep pacing tight so the video does not feel like random footage over audio. My quote includes [deliverable], [revision rounds], and [timeline]. I would need the script, voiceover, brand notes, and any asset rules. Samples: [links].

Faceless clients care about judgement and licensing, so mention both.

## Template for a recurring retainer

> Hi [name], this looks like a good fit for an ongoing weekly workflow. I would suggest starting with a paid trial batch of [number] clips. If the style is right, we can move to a weekly package: [number] clips, [turnaround], [revision rules], and a fixed rate of [price]. That keeps delivery predictable and avoids renegotiating every batch.

Retainer proposals should make the creator feel that you can handle process, not just editing.

## Mistakes that lose jobs

Avoid:

- Sending only "I am interested"
- Listing every software tool without explaining the result
- Ignoring the creator's niche
- Quoting without clarifying source review
- Promising "viral" edits
- Linking irrelevant samples
- Writing a huge wall of text

The proposal should make the creator confident enough to open your portfolio.

## How to price inside a proposal

If the job has a budget, respond within that range or explain what you can do for it. If the job is unclear, quote a trial batch and define assumptions.

Example:

> For $200, I can deliver five 30-60 second clips from timestamped footage, with captions and one revision round. Full episode review would be a separate scope.

That protects you from accidentally accepting unpaid review time.

## Follow-up message template

If a creator replies with interest, do not jump straight into vague enthusiasm. Confirm the next step.

> Thanks, this sounds good. To confirm the trial scope: [number] clips from [source], [length] each, with [captions/export specs], delivered by [date], including [revision rounds], for [price]. Once you send the footage and style references, I can start.

This prevents confusion before work begins. It also makes you look organised, which matters as much as editing skill when creators are hiring for recurring work.

## Proposal checklist

Before sending, confirm:

- You used the creator's content type
- You linked relevant samples
- You defined what is included
- You named timeline or availability
- You asked for the files you need
- You avoided promising guaranteed views
- You kept it short enough to read quickly

If the proposal could be sent unchanged to any job, make it more specific.

Save your best proposals. Over time, you will build a small library for podcasts, Reels, gaming, faceless videos, and retainers. Reuse the structure, but always customise the first lines, scope, and samples for the exact job.

## FAQ

### Should I customise every proposal?

Yes, but not from scratch. Use templates, then customise the first two sentences and the relevant samples.

### How many samples should I send?

Send two or three relevant samples. More is not better if the links are off-niche.

### Should I offer a free test edit?

Offer a paid trial batch instead. It respects your time and attracts more serious clients.

### What should I do before applying?

Make sure your portfolio has niche-specific examples. If you do not have clients yet, read [how to build a portfolio before you have clients](/guides/video-editing-portfolio-no-clients).
