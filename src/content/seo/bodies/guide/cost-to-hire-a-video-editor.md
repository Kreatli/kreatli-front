# How Much Does It Cost to Hire a Video Editor in 2026?

Hiring a freelance video editor for short-form clipping typically costs **$15–$75 per finished clip**, or **$800–$3,500+ per month** on retainer — depending on niche, complexity, and volume. On **Kreatli Marketplace**, you agree fixed project or milestone rates upfront instead of chasing per-view payouts or surprise agency markups.

This guide breaks down real 2026 pricing so you can budget before you post a job — whether you need podcast clips, YouTube Shorts, or TikTok-native edits delivered to your channels.

## What drives video editing cost?

Four variables move the price more than any platform fee:

1. **Deliverable type** — a simple talking-head podcast clip costs less than a gaming highlight with sound design and motion graphics.
2. **Turnaround** — same-day or 24-hour rush batches command premium rates.
3. **Volume** — per-clip pricing drops when you commit to 20–40 clips per week.
4. **Editor experience** — niche specialists (finance podcasts, competitive gaming) charge more because bad edits cost you credibility.

Generalist "video editing" gigs on open marketplaces often hide clipping inexperience behind low prices. A $10 clip that needs three revision rounds is not cheaper than a $30 clip that ships right.

## Per-clip pricing benchmarks (2026)

| --- | --- | --- |
| Podcast talking-head | $20–$40 | Jump cuts, captions, 30–90 sec vertical |
| YouTube Shorts (repurposed) | $15–$35 | Sourced from long-form uploads |

**Trial batches** are standard: pay for 5–10 clips before negotiating a weekly retainer.

## Retainer and monthly pricing

Creators posting daily or near-daily often switch from per-clip to weekly/monthly agreements:

| Volume | Rough monthly range | Structure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 20 clips/week | $800–$1,800 | One editor, consistent style |
| 40 clips/week | $1,500–$3,500 | May need backup editor at peak |
| 3 long-form + 30 Shorts | $2,000–$4,500 | Mixed scope — clarify in brief |

Retainers work when your editor knows your brand voice. Post a clear SOP (caption rules, hook examples, do-not-use sections) to avoid revision loops that eat savings.

## Hidden costs to budget for

- **Revision rounds** — agree how many are included (usually 1–2 per clip).
- **Source file access** — Dropbox/Google Drive organisation saves editor time; messy libraries cost you.
- **Music licensing** — royalty-free is standard; premium tracks may be a separate line item.
- **Platform fees** — compare total cost on specialist marketplaces vs generalist gigs with high seller fees. See [Kreatli vs. Fiverr](/compare/kreatli-vs-fiverr) and [Kreatli vs. Upwork](/compare/kreatli-vs-upwork) for context.

## FAQ

### Is $15 per clip too cheap to be good?

Not always for simple repurposed talking-head content at high volume — but verify samples. Suspiciously low rates on open marketplaces often mean template edits or unlimited revision chaos. Pay for a **small trial** before scaling.

### Do editors charge per hour or per clip?

Clipping workflows are almost always **per deliverable** (per clip, per episode batch, or weekly retainer). Hourly billing suits exploratory long-form projects, not 30 Shorts per week.

### Should I pay per view instead of per clip?

Per-view models belong to **content-rewards clipping** (clipper posts on their account). If you want clips on **your** channel with predictable cost, hire for fixed project pay. See [clipping marketplace vs video editor marketplace](/compare/clipping-marketplace-vs-video-editor-marketplace).

### How do I know if my budget is realistic?

Post your job with honest numbers. If you get zero qualified applicants, raise the rate or reduce scope (fewer motion graphics, longer turnaround). If you get flooded with off-niche replies, tighten your brief and use a clipping-focused marketplace.
