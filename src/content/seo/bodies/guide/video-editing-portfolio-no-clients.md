# How to Build a Portfolio Before You Have Clients

You do not need paid clients to build a video editing portfolio. You need proof that you can make useful edits for the kind of creators you want to work with. **Kreatli Marketplace** lets clip editors create profiles and apply to jobs, but your profile works much better when it shows clear, niche-specific samples.

This guide shows how to build a portfolio from scratch without pretending you have client work.

## Pick a niche first

A beginner portfolio fails when it tries to prove everything. Choose one or two editing lanes:

- Podcast clips
- YouTube Shorts
- TikTok/Reels talking-head clips
- Gaming highlights
- Twitch VOD clips
- Faceless channel edits
- Fitness, beauty, finance, real estate, or education clips

Niche samples make it easier for creators to imagine hiring you. Five relevant clips beat one generic montage.

## Build 3-5 sample projects

Your first portfolio can be simple:

1. A talking-head clip with captions
2. A podcast clip with two speakers
3. A vertical clip from long-form YouTube footage
4. A niche-specific sample, like gaming or fitness
5. A before-and-after edit showing raw footage vs final result

Do not use copyrighted footage in a way that creates risk. Use your own recordings, public practice footage, properly licensed stock, or footage from creators who allow practice edits. When in doubt, label the project as a practice sample and do not imply it was paid client work.

## Where to get practice material

You can create strong samples without client footage.

Options include:

- Record yourself explaining a topic for 60 seconds
- Ask a friend with a podcast or channel for permission
- Use public-domain footage where appropriate
- Use licensed stock video for faceless samples
- Edit your own gameplay or livestream recordings
- Create a screen-recorded tutorial
- Use practice footage from editing communities if licensing allows portfolio use

Always keep a note of where footage came from. If a creator asks whether the work was paid client work, be honest. "Practice sample built to show podcast clipping style" is perfectly acceptable when the edit is strong.

## Build samples around buyer intent

Creators do not search portfolios the way editors do. They ask, "Can this person solve my problem?" Build samples for common buyer problems:

- "I have a podcast and need clips."
- "I stream but never post highlights."
- "I need Reels with clean captions."
- "I run a faceless channel and need visuals."
- "I want Shorts from long YouTube videos."

Name your samples accordingly. "Podcast clip with captions" is more useful than "Edit 03."

## Make before-and-after examples

Before-and-after examples are powerful because they show your judgement. Put a short raw segment next to the finished clip and explain what you changed:

- Removed pauses
- Reframed to 9:16
- Added captions
- Tightened the hook
- Added B-roll
- Balanced audio
- Cut the ending for a stronger loop

Creators hire editors to solve problems. Show the problem and the fix.

## Write mini case studies

Each portfolio item should have a short note:

- Source type
- Goal
- What you edited
- Tools used
- Turnaround estimate
- What kind of creator this would help

Example:

> Podcast clip practice sample. Goal: turn a 90-second answer into a 42-second Reel. I removed repeated phrases, added readable captions, reframed both speakers for vertical, and opened on the strongest claim.

This tells clients how you think, not just what the final file looks like.

## Create samples for real job posts

Read job posts in your target niche and make practice samples that match them. If creators keep asking for "10 Shorts per week from podcasts," build a podcast clipping sample. If gaming creators ask for stream highlights, build a highlight sample.

Your portfolio should answer the market, not your personal editing taste only.

## Keep the portfolio easy to review

Creators are busy. Make your portfolio scannable:

- Put your best three samples first
- Label each sample by niche and deliverable
- Include clip length and platform
- Avoid giant downloads
- Use links that work on mobile
- Add a short note under each sample

Do not make people watch a two-minute montage to understand whether you can edit a 30-second Short.

## What to put on your Kreatli profile

On your profile, focus on outcomes:

- "Podcast clips for founders and coaches"
- "Gaming highlights from Twitch VODs"
- "Captioned Reels for education creators"
- "Faceless YouTube edits with stock and B-roll"

List your tools, but do not lead with tools only. Creators care more about whether you understand their content and can deliver reliably.

## Common beginner mistakes

Avoid:

- Posting only anime or music edits if you want creator clients
- Using copyrighted music without thinking
- Claiming practice edits are client work
- Showing too many styles with no focus
- Making captions too small
- Overediting every clip
- Hiding your best sample in a long reel

Your portfolio should make the hiring decision easier.

## Portfolio page checklist

Before applying to jobs, make sure your portfolio has:

- 3-5 relevant samples
- Clear labels by niche
- Short notes explaining your decisions
- Mobile-friendly links
- No broken permissions
- Honest practice/client labels
- Your availability and preferred work type
- A simple way to contact or hire you

If your portfolio takes more than one minute to understand, simplify it. The goal is not to show everything you can do. The goal is to make the right creator trust you enough to start a paid trial.

Update the portfolio as soon as you complete real projects. Replace weaker practice samples with paid work, but keep the same structure: niche label, short context, your editing decisions, and the result. A portfolio should get clearer as you gain experience, not larger and harder to review.

## FAQ

### Can I use footage from YouTube to practice?

You can practice privately, but be careful about public portfolio use. Prefer your own footage, licensed assets, public-domain material, or permission from creators.

### How many samples do I need?

Three strong niche samples are enough to start applying. Add more as you learn.

### Should I work for free to get samples?

Free work can become messy. A better path is practice samples, then a low-risk paid trial batch for a real creator.

### What next?

Build three focused samples, write short notes for each, then create your clipper profile and start applying to jobs that match your niche.
