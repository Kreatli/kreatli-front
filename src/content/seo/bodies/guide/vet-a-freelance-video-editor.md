# How to Vet a Freelance Video Editor Before Hiring

Vetting a freelance video editor means verifying they can deliver **your** niche, **your** pacing, and **your** turnaround — not just a polished demo reel from unrelated work. Before you commit to a weekly clipping pipeline, review samples, run a paid trial, and check how they communicate under revision pressure.

## Why vetting matters more for clipping than general editing

Clip editing is repetitive and brand-sensitive. A great wedding filmmaker might butcher podcast hooks. A fast TikTok editor might miss compliance rules for finance or health content. Bad vetting shows up as:

- Hooks that bury the payoff after 5 seconds
- Captions that drift off-sync after the first cut
- Inconsistent framing week to week
- Missed deadlines when you scale from 5 to 25 clips

One paid trial batch is cheaper than a month of unusable deliverables.

## Step 1: Define your non-negotiables

Before browsing profiles, write a one-page checklist:

- **Niche** — podcast, gaming, coaching, ecom, etc.
- **Output** — 9:16, length range, caption style (burned-in, keyword highlight)
- **Volume** — clips per week and turnaround (e.g. 48 hours)
- **Source** — how you share files (Drive, Dropbox, frame.io)
- **Revision policy** — how many rounds included

Share this in your job post so editors self-filter.

## Step 2: Review portfolios with a clipping lens

Do not grade on cinematic colour alone. Look for:

1. **Vertical samples** — at least 3 clips in 9:16, not only horizontal showreels
2. **Hook timing** — does something interesting happen in the first 2 seconds?
3. **Caption quality** — readable on mobile, correct spelling, on-beat cuts
4. **Niche match** — podcast talking-head if you run a podcast; gameplay if you stream
5. **Consistency** — multiple clips that feel like the same brand

Red flags: only template spam, stock mashups with no source context, or zero clips published in the last 6 months.

## Step 3: Run a paid trial (not free "tests")

Request **5–10 clips** on a real episode or footage batch — paid at your agreed rate. Free tests attract volume applicants and disrespect professional editors.

Evaluate the trial on:

| Criterion | What good looks like |
| --- | --- |
| Deadline | Delivered on time without reminders |
| Brief adherence | Clip count, length, and style match |
| Revision response | Thoughtful fixes within 24 hours |
| Communication | Asks smart questions upfront, not after deadline |
| File hygiene | Named files, correct aspect ratio, no watermarks |

One weak trial clip among five is normal. A pattern of missed hooks is not.

## Step 4: Check references and platform history

- **Ratings and completed jobs** on the marketplace (if available on profile)
- **Repeat hire signals** — creators who rebook the same editor
- **Scope honesty** — do they decline work outside their niche?

On generalist platforms, also watch for gig descriptions that promise "unlimited revisions in 24 hours" — that often signals race-to-bottom pricing.

## Step 5: Agree terms before scaling

Before a retainer, confirm in writing:

- Price per clip, per episode, or weekly batch
- Turnaround and time zone overlap
- Who licenses music and stock assets
- Confidentiality for unreleased content
- Exit clause — how either side ends the retainer with notice

## FAQ

### How many sample clips should I ask for?

**3–5 vertical samples** in your niche is enough for screening. The real decision should come from a **paid trial** on your footage — samples can be cherry-picked.

### What questions should I ask in a kickoff call?

- "How do you find hooks in a 90-minute podcast?"
- "What's your average turnaround for 10 clips?"
- "How do you handle revision requests?"
- "Have you worked with [your niche] before?"

Listen for specifics, not generic promises.

### Should I hire the cheapest qualified editor?

Lowest bid often costs more in revisions and missed deadlines. Pick the best **trial deliverable per dollar** — not the lowest rate on paper.

### Can I vet an editor through a content-rewards clipping program instead?

Content-rewards programs optimise for clipper distribution and views — different skill set from delivering polished files to **your** channel. Vet editors on the model you actually need: [what is a clipper](/glossary/what-is-a-clipper).
