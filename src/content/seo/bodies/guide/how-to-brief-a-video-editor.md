# How to Brief a Video Editor

A good editor can only do so much with a vague brief. If you want clips that match your voice, platform, and audience, you need to give the editor enough context to make smart choices. **Kreatli Marketplace** helps creators hire clip editors, but the best results come from clear briefs before the first batch starts.

This guide gives you a practical briefing system for Shorts, TikToks, Reels, podcasts, livestreams, and other creator content.

## What a video editing brief should do

A brief is not a long brand document. It is a working instruction sheet that helps the editor answer:

- What is the goal of the clip?
- Who is the audience?
- What source footage should be used?
- What style should the edit follow?
- What deliverables are required?
- What should the editor avoid?
- How will feedback work?

The goal is to reduce guessing. Every unanswered question becomes either a delay or a revision.

## The one-page brief structure

Use this structure for most clipping jobs:

1. **Project goal** - Why are you making these clips?
2. **Audience** - Who should care?
3. **Source files** - Where is the footage and what matters?
4. **Deliverables** - How many clips, how long, what format?
5. **Style references** - What should it feel like?
6. **Rules** - Captions, music, words to avoid, brand assets.
7. **Review process** - Deadline, revision rounds, feedback format.

You can write this in a doc, Notion page, spreadsheet, or job post. What matters is clarity.

## Define the goal

"Make this engaging" is not enough. Tell the editor what the clip is supposed to achieve.

Examples:

- Get viewers to watch the full podcast
- Grow YouTube Shorts reach
- Turn webinar answers into LinkedIn clips
- Make a coach's advice feel clear and credible
- Create funny stream highlights for TikTok
- Test hooks for a product launch

Different goals create different edits. A retention clip and a sales clip need different endings.

## Share useful source context

Do not drop a folder of files with no explanation. Include:

- Episode or stream title
- Best timestamps
- Sections to avoid
- Sponsor reads or private comments to remove
- Names and roles of speakers
- Correct pronunciation for names or brands
- Transcript, if available

For podcasts and livestreams, timestamps are the biggest time saver. Even rough notes help.

## Give style references

Send three examples:

1. One clip that matches the pacing you want.
2. One clip with caption style you like.
3. One clip that shows what to avoid.

Then explain why. Editors cannot read your taste from links alone.

Good notes:

- "Use captions like this: large, white, high contrast."
- "I like the first two seconds because it opens on a strong claim."
- "Avoid this many sound effects; it feels too chaotic for my brand."
- "Keep the edit cleaner, like a founder interview, not a meme page."

## Specify deliverables

Write deliverables like a checklist:

- 8 clips
- 30-60 seconds each
- 9:16 vertical
- Burned-in captions
- Export as MP4
- Versions for TikTok/Reels/Shorts if needed
- One revision round
- Delivery by Friday

If you need title suggestions, thumbnail frames, caption files, or upload copy, say so. Do not assume they are included.

## Feedback rules

Feedback can make or break the relationship. Give specific notes tied to timecodes.

Weak feedback:

> Make it pop more.

Useful feedback:

> At 0:03, cut the pause before "the real problem." At 0:12, the caption covers the product. At 0:22, remove the zoom because it makes the serious point feel too casual.

Agree on revision limits before work starts. One or two rounds are normal. Unlimited revisions create frustration for both sides.

## Create a reusable style guide

Once you have worked with an editor for a few batches, turn repeated feedback into a short style guide. This saves time when you rehire, add a second editor, or scale production.

Your style guide can include:

- Caption font, size, and colour
- Safe zones for TikTok, Reels, and Shorts
- Intro hook rules
- How aggressive cuts should feel
- Music volume preferences
- Words or phrases to avoid
- How to handle brand names
- Examples of approved clips
- Examples of rejected clips

Keep it practical. A five-page brand manifesto is less useful than three approved clips with notes.

## Briefing for different content types

Adjust the brief by source:

| Source | Extra context to include |
| --- | --- |
| Podcast | Speaker names, timestamps, episode theme |
| Livestream | Sections to avoid, chat context, best moments |
| Gaming | Game, UI safe zones, meme style, audio tracks |
| Faceless video | Script, voiceover, stock rules, visual references |
| Product content | Claims, forbidden wording, brand assets |

The same editor may handle multiple formats, but they should not receive the same brief for every format.

## What to send after approval

Once you choose an editor, send one clean package instead of scattered messages:

- Final source links
- The agreed brief
- Brand assets
- Examples
- Deadline
- Payment milestone
- Feedback location

This makes the project feel professional from the first day. It also gives you one place to check if something goes wrong. Most revision problems start because instructions were split across DMs, comments, and old files.

## Brief template

> Goal: [what the clips should achieve]  
> Audience: [who watches]  
> Source: [link to footage, transcript, timestamps]  
> Deliverables: [number, length, format, platforms]  
> Style: [links and notes]  
> Must include: [captions, B-roll, CTA, brand assets]  
> Avoid: [topics, music, effects, sections]  
> Deadline: [date]  
> Feedback: [where and how revisions happen]

Keep this template and reuse it for every editor.

## FAQ

### Do I need a detailed brief for a trial batch?

Yes, but keep it short. A trial should test editing skill, not the editor's ability to guess your taste.

### What if I want the editor to choose moments?

Say that explicitly and pay for source review. Give examples of what counts as a strong moment.

### Should the brief live in the job post?

Put the essentials in the job post, then share a fuller brief after you choose an editor.
