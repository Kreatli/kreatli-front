# How to Find a TikTok or Reels Editor

The right TikTok or Reels editor is not just someone who can cut video. You need someone who understands hooks, vertical pacing, captions, trends, and why a clip has to make sense without the full context. **Kreatli Marketplace** helps creators find editors who specialise in short-form content instead of forcing you to sort through generic video portfolios.

This guide shows where to look, what to test, and how to hire without wasting weeks on the wrong editor.

## Start with the job you actually need done

"I need a TikTok editor" is too vague. Before you search, define the work:

- Are clips repurposed from podcasts, streams, webinars, or long YouTube videos?
- Do you need native TikTok content filmed for trends?
- Will the editor choose moments, or will you provide timestamps?
- Do you need captions, B-roll, memes, motion graphics, or only clean cuts?
- How many clips per week?
- Will one edit be adapted for TikTok, Reels, and Shorts?

Editors price and apply based on scope. A creator who provides timestamps and asks for five captioned clips per week is hiring for a different job than a creator who wants the editor to review six hours of footage and find every moment.

## What a strong portfolio looks like

Do not only look for flashy effects. Good short-form editing is clear, fast, and intentional.

Strong samples usually show:

- A hook in the first one to three seconds
- Captions that are readable on a phone
- Cuts that remove dead air without ruining personality
- Platform-safe text placement
- A clear payoff or reason to keep watching
- Examples from a similar niche
- Multiple clips with consistent style

If an editor only sends cinematic travel reels or wedding videos, they may be talented but unproven for creator clipping. Ask for vertical examples.

## Run a paid trial batch

Do not hire a long-term editor from one message. Use a paid trial batch.

A good trial looks like this:

1. Send one source video or a folder of clips.
2. Provide two or three examples of the style you want.
3. Ask for three finished edits.
4. Include one revision round.
5. Judge the work on moment choice, pacing, captions, and communication.

Paying for the trial matters. Good editors are more likely to take the test seriously, and you get a realistic preview of the working relationship.

## Questions to ask before hiring

Ask practical questions:

- What short-form platforms do you edit for most?
- Can you show clips from my niche or a similar one?
- Do you prefer timestamps or full source review?
- How many clips can you deliver per week?
- What caption style do you recommend for my content?
- How many revisions are included?
- Can you adapt one clip for TikTok, Reels, and Shorts?
- What do you need from me before starting?

The best editors will ask you questions too. If someone accepts every requirement instantly with no scope discussion, slow down.

## Budget expectations

Straightforward vertical clips often cost **$20-$45 per clip**. Heavier edits with B-roll, sound design, visual effects, or full source review cost more. Weekly batches or retainers can reduce per-clip friction once the editor knows your style.

If your budget is tight, reduce scope before you reduce quality. Provide timestamps, simplify captions, use fewer effects, or start with fewer clips per week. A cheap edit that needs three revisions is not cheap.

## Compare applicants fairly

When you receive applications, do not choose only by price. Build a simple scorecard:

| Criterion | What to check |
| --- | --- |
| Relevant samples | Do they show TikTok, Reels, or Shorts work? |
| Hook judgement | Does the first second create curiosity? |
| Caption quality | Are captions readable and well placed? |
| Niche fit | Have they edited similar creators or formats? |
| Communication | Did they respond to your actual job post? |
| Process | Can they explain source review, revisions, and delivery? |

The strongest applicant is often not the cheapest or the flashiest. It is the editor who understands your content type and can repeat the workflow every week.

## What to do after the first batch

After the trial batch, review performance and process separately. A clip can underperform for reasons outside the editor's control, but the workflow should still feel organised.

Ask:

- Did the editor deliver on time?
- Did they follow the brief?
- Were revisions easy?
- Did they choose strong moments?
- Did captions and exports work on every platform?
- Did they suggest improvements?

If the answer is mostly yes, move to a recurring batch. If the edits are close but not perfect, update your brief before hiring someone new. Many creator-editor relationships improve dramatically after the first feedback loop.

## FAQ

### Should I hire one editor for TikTok, Reels, and Shorts?

Usually, yes. The same editor can make a master vertical cut and adapt it for each platform if you specify the exports.

### What if I do not know which moments to clip?

Hire someone who includes source review and moment selection. Expect to pay more than if you provide timestamps.

### How do I know if an editor understands retention?

Look at the first five seconds of their samples. If the opening is slow, unclear, or purely decorative, keep looking.

### What is the fastest way to start?

Post a focused job with your niche, source footage, weekly volume, examples, and trial budget. Then compare applicants by relevant short-form samples, not generic editing ability.
