# How to Write a Job Post That Attracts Good Clip Editors

Good clip editors skip vague job posts. If your post says "need video editor for TikTok, DM rates," qualified editors cannot tell whether the job is a quick caption pass, full VOD review, a weekly retainer, or a chaotic revision loop. **Kreatli Marketplace** works best when creators post clear clipping jobs with scope, examples, budget, and deliverables.

Use this guide to write a job post that attracts editors who can actually do the work.

## The core job post formula

A strong video editing job post answers six questions:

1. What kind of content do you make?
2. What source footage will the editor receive?
3. What finished deliverables do you need?
4. What style should the edit follow?
5. What is the timeline and volume?
6. What is the budget or pricing structure?

If any of those are missing, good editors have to guess. Guessing creates bad quotes, mismatched applicants, and slow hiring.

## Start with the content type

Name your niche in the first sentence. "Video editor needed" is weaker than "Podcast clip editor needed for a weekly B2B founder interview show."

Useful examples:

- YouTube Shorts editor for a fitness education channel
- TikTok editor for a beauty creator
- Twitch VOD editor for gaming highlights
- Podcast clip editor for founder interviews
- Faceless YouTube editor for finance explainers
- Instagram Reels editor for a coaching brand

Niche matters because editing style changes by audience. A gaming highlight, finance clip, and skincare Reel should not feel the same.

## Define the source footage

Editors need to know what they are starting from.

Specify:

- Raw talking-head footage
- Long-form YouTube videos
- Podcast recordings
- Twitch or livestream VODs
- Product footage
- Screen recordings
- Script and voiceover
- Timestamped moments

Also mention source length. Reviewing a 20-minute video is very different from reviewing a four-hour livestream.

## Define deliverables

Be concrete:

- Number of clips per week
- Clip length
- Aspect ratio
- Captions included or not
- B-roll included or not
- Platform versions
- Thumbnails or title frames
- Revision rounds
- File format

Example:

> 10 vertical clips per week, 30-60 seconds each, 9:16, burned-in captions, exported for TikTok/Reels/Shorts, one revision round included.

This prevents applicants from underquoting and then discovering the job is twice as large.

## Make the first project easy to quote

If you want strong editors to apply, make the first project concrete. "Ongoing work available" is fine, but the first milestone should be specific.

Better:

> Trial batch: five 30-60 second clips from one 45-minute podcast episode. I will provide timestamps, transcript, and style examples. One revision round included.

Weaker:

> Need someone long term for content. Send rates.

A clear trial attracts better applicants because they can estimate time, price, and fit. It also protects you from committing to a retainer before you know whether the editor understands your content.

## Tell editors how you will choose

Add one sentence about your hiring process. For example:

> I will choose based on relevant short-form samples, understanding of the brief, and a paid trial batch.

This signals that you are serious and discourages low-effort applications. It also tells good editors what to send: not every project they have ever made, but the two or three samples that match your job.

## Add style references

Style references are more useful than adjectives. "Fast-paced but not too flashy" means different things to different editors.

Include:

- Two clips you like
- One clip you dislike
- Notes about why
- Caption style preferences
- Brand colours or fonts
- Any effects to avoid

If you do not have exact references, use your own past content and explain what should improve: stronger hooks, cleaner captions, faster cuts, more B-roll, or less visual clutter.

## Include a budget range

A budget range saves everyone time. You do not need to know the perfect price, but editors need to know whether the job is realistic.

For short-form clipping, common structures include:

- Per clip
- Per episode batch
- Per VOD
- Weekly retainer
- Monthly retainer
- Paid trial batch

If you are unsure, start with a paid trial. For example:

> Budget: $150-$250 for a trial batch of five clips. If the fit is good, I want an ongoing weekly batch.

For pricing context, read [cost to hire a video editor](/guides/cost-to-hire-a-video-editor).

## Example job post

> I need a short-form editor for a weekly podcast about creator business. Each episode is 45-60 minutes. I will provide the recording, transcript, and 5-8 suggested timestamps. I need 8 vertical clips per week, 30-60 seconds each, exported for YouTube Shorts, TikTok, and Instagram Reels. Please add readable burned-in captions, remove dead air, and create hooks that make sense without the full episode. Style references: [links]. Budget is $250-$400 for the first batch, with weekly work available if the fit is strong.

That post gives editors enough information to self-select and quote honestly.

## Red flags to avoid

Avoid phrases like:

- "Easy job"
- "Should only take a few minutes"
- "Looking for cheap editor"
- "Exposure opportunity"
- "Need viral edits"
- "Unlimited revisions"

These phrases repel experienced editors. If you want quality applicants, show that you understand the work.

## Final pre-publish checklist

Before posting, confirm your job includes:

- Niche and content type
- Source footage length
- Number of deliverables
- Platforms and aspect ratio
- Caption and B-roll expectations
- Timeline
- Budget or trial budget
- Style references
- Revision expectations
- How applicants should respond

If the post answers those points, you are much more likely to attract editors who can quote accurately and start quickly.

One more useful detail: tell editors what a successful first week looks like. For example, "I want five clips delivered by Friday, with the first two approved before the remaining three are finished." That small process note prevents an editor from completing a full batch in the wrong style.

## FAQ

### Should I ask for a free test edit?

Use a paid trial instead. It attracts better editors and gives you a more realistic preview.

### What if I do not know my exact style yet?

Say that. Ask for a trial batch with two style variations, but keep the scope small.

### Can I hire without a budget?

You can, but you will get noisier applications. A range helps editors decide whether to apply.

### What should I do next?

Create a clipping job with your niche, source footage, deliverables, examples, and budget. The clearer the post, the easier it is for strong editors to say yes.
